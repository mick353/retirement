Estate analysis at key ages

Why the real number is higher than the worst-case lines

Four structural factors mean actual death tax is materially lower than the ceiling shown. Each pushes the true after-tax estate toward (or above) the green mid-case line.
1 · Spouse / dependant exemption
DBT can be $0
Super paid to a spouse or other tax dependant attracts no death benefits tax at all, regardless of the taxable component. The 17% only applies to adult non-dependants (e.g. independent children).
2 · Tax-free super component
Only part is taxed
The PSS lump sum and Hostplus carry a tax-free component that passes to anyone untaxed. The worst-case cards assume 100% taxable; the mid-case assumes ~71%. Real DBT applies only to the taxable slice.
3 · CGT is on the gain, not the balance
Far less than 23.5%
Pool C CGT applies only to unrealised gains, not the whole balance. On death, assets transfer at your cost base — no CGT event is triggered then; beneficiaries inherit the cost base and pay only when they sell, at their own rate and discount.
4 · Late-life restructuring
Withdraw tax-free
Over 60 you can withdraw from super tax-free while alive, converting it to personal cash that escapes DBT entirely. A re-contribution or simple drawdown strategy in late life can sharply reduce the super DBT exposure shown here.
Model parameters. Worst-case (ceiling): DBT 17% on 100% of super; Pool C CGT 23.5% (47% marginal × 50% discount) on the full balance. Realistic mid-case: DBT 17% on ~71% taxable super; Pool C CGT 15% (30% marginal × 50% discount) on ~50% of the balance assumed to be gain. These are planning illustrations, not tax advice — your actual position depends on your taxable/tax-free split, who inherits, and any restructuring. Pool C distribution-tax drag is already reflected in the balance trajectory. Confirm specifics with a licensed adviser.
